CAMS Competition

About

The CAMS competition aims to incentivize and inspire cities to actively engage in reducing emissions from burning fossil fuels, encouraging a movement towards cleaner air and a sustainable future.

Society rewards progress and innovation in various areas of life. Extending this concept to address climate change, we should reward efforts to combat the global challenge. Nitrogen dioxide (NO2) from burning fossil fuels is a visible marker of the climate emergency, impacting air quality and human health. By reducing NO2 emissions, we improve air quality, protect health, and tackle the root cause of climate change. This project aims to implement an NO2 index for city comparisons and visualize the data to inspire NO2 emission reduction.

The code is available on GitHub.

Data

The dataset used in this project provides air quality information for 50 European and Turkish cities. It incorporates satellite and ground-based observations, along with advanced numerical models, and is generated by the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service (CAMS) under the EU's Earth observation program. The data examines background NO2 levels in each city.

Raw data representation

The raw data representation shows the inherent fluctuations and seasonality of NO2 levels, influenced by factors like traffic, weather, and industry. Winter months often exhibit higher concentrations. This unfiltered depiction offers insights into real-time variations, showcasing the immediate impact of local emissions and external factors on air quality. By observing this chart, we can gain a understanding of NO2 dynamics across the dataset's 50 cities.
Source: CAMS, Observable.

Deseasonalization

The second chart offers a deseasonalized view of NO2 data, providing insights into medium-term trends across the dataset's 50 cities. A composite moving average calculation is applied, combining different timeframes to create a balanced indicator. The dropdown menu allows you to choose between the composite moving average (weighted 365 days, 2 years, and 3 years) and the 365-day moving average. This deseasonalized data reveals patterns beyond immediate fluctuations, offering a clearer understanding of NO2 level trajectories over time. It helps identify consistent improvements or deteriorations and facilitates the evaluation of medium-term efforts to reduce NO2 emissions.


Source: CAMS, Observable, own calculations.

A contest to slash NO2 levels

The third chart introduces a contest among the dataset's 50 cities, focused on reducing NO2 levels. The competition allows you to choose between yearly and monthly time frames and tracks changes in NO2 levels over time. At the end of the period, the city with the most significant reduction is crowned the champion.
Source: CAMS, Observable, own calculations.

This competition could encourage cities to actively work towards reducing NO2 emissions, fostering friendly rivalry and collective progress. It showcases success stories, inspires healthy competition, and empowers cities to make a tangible difference in their local environments. It spurs innovation, collaboration, and contributes to the broader goal of combating climate change.

Outlook

The visualization offers a pathway to support and reward local environmental NGOs. It enables fundraising campaigns, promotes community engagement, and showcases the progress made in reducing NO2 emissions. By expanding the competition aspect, we can incentivize and recognize achievements by these organizations. This hopefully creates a cycle of progress and support, driving towards a cleaner, healthier, and more sustainable future. Satellite data enables global expansion beyond city limits.
